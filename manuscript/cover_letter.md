# Cover Letter

Dear Editor,

We submit our manuscript entitled **"Proteogenomic analysis identifies IRAG1 as a genetically validated therapeutic target for migraine through the cGMP-PKG-Ca2+ signaling axis"** for consideration for publication in Nature Genetics.

Migraine affects over one billion people worldwide, yet approximately 30% of patients derive inadequate benefit from current CGRP-targeted therapies. Identifying mechanistically distinct therapeutic targets — particularly those supported by human genetic evidence, which doubles the probability of clinical trial success — represents an urgent priority. In this study, we apply a comprehensive proteogenomic framework integrating two-stage plasma proteome Mendelian randomization, Bayesian colocalization, fine-mapping, epigenomic annotation, single-cell transcriptomics, and phenome-wide safety assessment to identify IRAG1 as a first-in-class, genetically validated therapeutic target for migraine.

Our key findings are:

1. **Strong protective causal effect**: Genetically predicted IRAG1 protein levels confer protection against migraine (IVW beta = -0.558, P = 5.84 x 10^-11), with the effect robust to all sensitivity analyses.

2. **Single-nucleotide causal resolution**: Colocalization (PP.H4 = 0.993) and fine-mapping (SuSiE PIP = 0.999) resolve the causal signal to rs4910165 within intron 1 of IRAG1.

3. **Functional mechanism**: rs4910165 resides in active regulatory chromatin (DNase I hypersensitive, H3K27ac+) specifically in vascular smooth muscle cells, where IRAG1 is most highly expressed. The variant regulates full-length IRAG1 containing both PRKG1 and ITPR1 interaction domains essential for cGMP-PKG-IP3R-Ca2+ signaling.

4. **Favorable safety profile**: Phenome-wide analysis reveals no adverse trait associations for the IRAG1-elevating allele. The cGMP-PKG pathway is pharmacologically validated by existing drugs (PDE5 inhibitors, sGC stimulators), yet IRAG1 itself represents a first-in-class target.

5. **Multi-layered validation**: Independent replication by Lou et al. (2024) using orthogonal SMR methodology, monogenic disease evidence (MRVI1 mutations cause achalasia), and convergent clinical pharmacology (PDE5i/GTN as headache triggers) provide external validation.

Our methodological framework builds directly on the two-stage pQTL MR approach recently published in Nature Genetics by Yoshiji et al. (2025, 57:345-357), extending this paradigm to neurovascular disease. We believe the convergent genetic, molecular, cellular, and pharmacological evidence — combined with the pressing unmet clinical need in migraine — makes this work well-suited for Nature Genetics.

This manuscript has not been published and is not under consideration elsewhere. All authors have approved the manuscript and agree with its submission to Nature Genetics.

We look forward to your consideration.
